About

I didn't set out to become a pricing strategist. I set out to understand how things work.

From 1996 to 2000 I studied Material Science at IIT Kanpur — with a minor in Computer Science, because I couldn't resist understanding how logical structures behave alongside physical ones. Two different languages for the same question: what makes a system do what it does? After graduating I joined Infosys in Bangalore as a Software Engineer, where I spent two years building trade processing systems for Goldman Sachs. I learned something there that has never left me — how quickly sophisticated technical work gets systematized, documented, and distributed across geographies the moment someone figures out the repeatable pattern. The offshore model was the original displacement engine. I was inside it at 22. It shaped everything I think about what knowledge is actually worth.

In 2002 I went to IIM Ahmedabad for my MBA in General Management, with an exchange semester at HHL Leipzig — my first real immersion in European commercial thinking and global business architecture. By the time I graduated in 2004 I had added strategic and commercial vocabulary to an engineering and systems foundation. What I needed next was a place where all three could work together.

Coca-Cola gave me the first piece. As an Area Sales Manager from 2004 to 2006, I learned what no engineering curriculum teaches — how to make people want something. The gap between what something costs to make and what someone will willingly pay for it is not a math problem. It is a human one. From 2006 to 2007 at Marketics in Bangalore, I ran rigorous, long-horizon analytics for businesses as a Domain Consultant. The answers were rarely what anyone expected. That combination — human intuition plus analytical precision — became my permanent operating mode.

Dell was my decade-long graduate school. From 2007 to 2012 across Dell India, and from 2012 to 2022 at Dell's headquarters in Round Rock, Texas, I built prediction models, collaborative filtering systems, and pricing architectures underneath some of the largest commercial decisions the company made. I moved from Consultant to Director over that decade. Pricing stopped being a function somewhere in that journey and became a craft — the point where strategy, psychology, data science, and human judgment all arrive at the same table simultaneously. The most honest test of whether a company actually understands the value it creates.

Since 2022 I have been at Cohesity as Senior Director of Pricing Strategy, leading a team of six through the company's transition to cloud-native SaaS monetization and a landmark industry integration. The question I work on every day is the same one that hooked me at Marketics: how do you build something worth exactly what it charges? The Knowledge Quadrant Framework and Kairos were inevitable. I watched knowledge work get commoditized at Infosys in 2000. I watched it happen again at Dell across a decade. Now I'm watching it happen to everyone, everywhere, at once — accelerated by AI. The framework is my attempt to give people a precise language for what is happening. Kairos is the tool that makes it personal.

The Knowledge Quadrant Framework

The question nobody is asking clearly enough is not "will AI take my job." It is "which part of what I do is actually mine."

The Knowledge Quadrant Framework maps how people create value at work — and how exposed that value is to AI displacement. Two axes. The first is the nature of your knowledge: Explicit knowledge is documented, trainable, searchable — the kind AI can replicate at scale. Tacit knowledge is judgment, pattern recognition, contextual instinct earned by living through real situations. You cannot Google it or prompt for it. The second axis is your output: Accuracy means there is a right answer and being wrong has real consequences. Originality means there is no right answer — the value lives entirely in the freshness of what you produce.

THE DOCTORS

Explicit knowledge + Accuracy required. Surgeons, lawyers, analysts, engineers writing production code. High stakes, verifiable output. AI is entering this room fastest.

THE BUILDERS

Explicit knowledge + Originality required. Marketers, designers, copywriters, product communicators. Pattern-based creativity at scale. AI is displacing the volume tier first.

THE GENERALS

Tacit knowledge + Accuracy required. Strategists, senior leaders, negotiators, advisors. Consequential calls on incomplete information. The human premium zone — and it is growing.

THE CREATORS

Tacit knowledge + Originality required. This quadrant creates history. AI can assist but cannot originate.

Within these four quadrants live eight personas — from The Doer (highest displacement risk) to The Oracle (minimal). Most people are not where they think they are. Kairos is built to close that gap.

Kairos

The moment of reckoning. Know where you stand.

I built Kairos because the conversation about AI and jobs is happening at the wrong altitude. Everyone is debating which industries will survive. Almost nobody is asking the harder question — within your specific day, within your specific work, how much of what you do is actually irreplaceable?

Kairos is a self-inquiry tool. You describe your day freely — no forms, no quizzes, no judgments about what you should have done. A local AI model reads your words through the Knowledge Quadrant Framework and sends you back an honest picture: your quadrant blend, your dominant persona, the displacement signals hiding in how you described your own work, and one question to sit with.

No login. No account. Your words never leave the machine. This is a mirror, not a test. Currently in private beta with a trusted network of peers.
